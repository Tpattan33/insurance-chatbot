## 1. About NJM Insurance Group

NJM Insurance Group is a regional, **mutual** property-and-casualty insurer. Because it is a mutual company, it is owned by its policyholders rather than outside shareholders, and underwriting profits are returned to policyholders as dividends rather than paid to stockholders.

**Quick facts**
- **Founded:** June 7, 1913, originally as the *New Jersey Manufacturers Casualty Insurance Company*.
- **Origin story:** Created to fulfill a pledge (associated with then–New Jersey Governor and future U.S. President Woodrow Wilson) to provide injured workers with medical care and partial wage replacement. NJM began as a workers' compensation carrier for members of the New Jersey Business & Industry Association (NJBIA), a relationship that still exists today.
- **Company type:** Mutual insurance group.
- **Headquarters:** West Trenton, New Jersey (301 Sullivan Way, West Trenton, NJ).
- **Offices:** Three locations — West Trenton, Hammonton, and Parsippany, New Jersey.
- **Employees:** Approximately 2,500.
- **President & CEO:** Carol L. Voorhees.
- **Website:** www.njm.com
- **Financial strength:** AM Best rating of **A+ (Superior/Excellent)**.
- **Customer satisfaction:** Consistently low complaint ratio with the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) — fewer complaints than expected for a company of its size — and recognition in J.D. Power auto claims studies/certification programs.

**Known limitations to mention to customers**
- NJM sells **personal** insurance in only five states (see Section 2).
- NJM historically has **no dedicated mobile app** for managing personal policies; customers use the mobile-friendly website instead.

## 5. NJM Dividends

Dividends are a signature part of NJM's value proposition as a mutual company.

- NJM has declared dividends to policyholders (not stockholders) **every year since 1918**.
- **Regular Dividends** — applied automatically as a **credit to the renewal premium** when a policy renews the following year (historically around 5% of the prior year's personal auto premium).
- **Special Dividends** — periodically paid **by check** in addition to Regular Dividends (historically calculated as a percentage of premiums paid in a prior year).
- New Jersey policyholders with NJM Auto, Homeowners, Condo, or Renters insurance are eligible for a Regular Dividend credit; NJM Auto policyholders may also receive a Special Dividend check.
- **Workers' compensation dividends** are credited to eligible commercial policyholders to help reduce premium.

> **Important caveat for the chatbot:** Dividends are **not guaranteed**, are not paid on all policy forms, and are not paid in every state in which NJM operates. Do not promise a dividend amount.

# GENERAL INSURANCE KNOWLEDGE

This section provides background concepts and state requirements so the chatbot can answer general "how does insurance work" questions and state-specific minimum-coverage questions for the states NJM serves.

## 8. Core Auto Insurance Concepts & Glossary

- **Liability insurance** — Pays for injuries and property damage you cause to *others*. It never pays for your own injuries or your own vehicle. It is the coverage most states legally require.
- **Bodily Injury (BI) liability** — Pays others' medical costs, lost wages, and related expenses when you cause an accident. Usually written as two numbers: per-person limit and per-accident limit.
- **Property Damage (PD) liability** — Pays to repair or replace others' property (their vehicle, a fence, a mailbox, a building) when you are at fault.
- **Split limits (e.g., 25/50/25)** — A shorthand for three liability limits, in thousands of dollars:
  - First number = BI per person (e.g., $25,000)
  - Second number = BI per accident (e.g., $50,000)
  - Third number = PD per accident (e.g., $25,000)
- **Personal Injury Protection (PIP)** — "No-fault" medical coverage that pays your own (and your passengers'/household members') medical expenses and sometimes lost wages after an accident, regardless of who caused it. Required in no-fault states.
- **First-Party Benefits (FPB) / Medical Benefits** — Pennsylvania's name for first-party medical coverage that functions similarly to PIP.
- **Uninsured Motorist (UM) coverage** — Pays for your injuries (and sometimes property damage) when an at-fault driver has *no* insurance, or in a hit-and-run.
- **Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage** — Pays when the at-fault driver has *some* insurance but not enough to cover your losses.
- **Uninsured Motorist Property Damage (UMPD)** — Covers damage to *your* vehicle caused by an uninsured at-fault driver (required in some states such as Maryland).
- **Collision coverage** — Pays to repair/replace *your* car after a collision, regardless of fault. Not legally required, but typically required by lenders/lessors.
- **Comprehensive coverage** — Pays for non-collision damage to your car (theft, fire, vandalism, weather, animal strikes). Not legally required, but typically required by lenders/lessors.
- **Deductible** — The amount you pay out of pocket on a comprehensive/collision claim before insurance pays the rest. Higher deductible = lower premium.
- **Premium** — The amount you pay for the policy (monthly, semiannually, or annually).
- **Limit** — The maximum an insurer will pay for a covered loss.

### Fault systems explained
- **At-fault (tort) state** — The driver who causes a crash (and their insurer) is responsible for the other party's injuries and damages. The injured party can generally sue the at-fault driver. (Examples among NJM states: CT, MD, OH.)
- **No-fault state** — Each driver's own PIP coverage pays their initial medical costs regardless of fault, and the right to sue for pain and suffering may be limited. (Example: NJ.)
- **Choice no-fault state** — Drivers choose between a limited-tort option (lower premium, restricted right to sue for pain and suffering) and a full-tort option (higher premium, full right to sue). (Example: PA.)

> **General recommendation the chatbot can share:** State *minimum* limits keep a driver legal but are often **not enough** to protect personal assets in a serious crash. Insurance professionals commonly recommend higher liability limits (for example, 100/300/100) and an umbrella policy for households with significant assets, teen drivers, pools, or frequent guests.

## 9. Core Home Insurance Concepts

A standard homeowners policy generally bundles several coverages:
- **Dwelling coverage** — Pays to repair or rebuild the home's structure (and attached structures like a porch) after a covered loss. Typically set to the cost to rebuild the home.
- **Other structures coverage** — Detached structures such as a fence, shed, or detached garage. Often about 10% of the dwelling limit.
- **Personal property coverage** — Belongings (furniture, electronics, clothing). Often 50%–70% of the dwelling limit. Can be written at **actual cash value** (depreciated) or **replacement cost** (cost to buy new).
- **Liability coverage** — If someone is injured on your property or you damage someone else's property.
- **Loss of use / additional living expenses** — Covers extra costs (e.g., a hotel) if your home is uninhabitable after a covered loss.
- **Open peril vs. named peril** — "Open peril" covers all causes of loss except those specifically excluded (broader); "named peril" covers only the causes specifically listed. NJM's HO-3 and HO-5 use open-peril coverage on the dwelling; HO-5 extends open-peril to personal property as well.
- **Flood is typically excluded** from standard home policies and must be purchased separately.

## 10. State Minimum Auto-Liability Requirements (NJM States)

> These are the **legal minimums** to drive in each state for the 2026 policy year. They are the floor, not a recommendation. UM/UIM rules and first-party medical rules differ by state.

### New Jersey (NJ) — No-fault state
New Jersey lets drivers choose between a **Basic Policy** and a **Standard Policy**, and raised its Standard Policy minimums effective **January 1, 2026** (the final phase of a two-phase increase under P.L. 2022, c. 87).

**Standard Policy — minimum liability (effective Jan 1, 2026): 35/70/25**
- Bodily Injury: **$35,000 per person** (raised from $25,000)
- Bodily Injury: **$70,000 per accident** (raised from $50,000)
- Property Damage: **$25,000 per accident** (unchanged)
- **PIP:** at least **$15,000 per person/per accident**, extending up to **$250,000** for certain severe injuries (e.g., brain or spinal cord injuries).
- **UM/UIM:** generally must match the policy's bodily injury liability limits.
- Standard Policy drivers choose a **limited right to sue** (lawsuit threshold) or an **unlimited right to sue** tort option.

**Basic Policy — reduced minimum coverage**
- Bodily Injury liability: **not included by default** (an optional $10,000 per accident can be added).
- Property Damage: **$5,000 per accident**.
- PIP: included but more limited than the Standard Policy.
- Comes with a **limited right to sue**.

> **Important:** Existing policies are automatically increased to the new Standard minimums at the first renewal after January 1, 2026. Insurance professionals strongly caution against the Basic Policy because it provides little or no bodily-injury liability protection.

---

### Pennsylvania (PA) — Choice no-fault state
**Minimum liability: 15/30/5**
- Bodily Injury: **$15,000 per person**
- Bodily Injury: **$30,000 per accident**
- Property Damage: **$5,000 per accident**
- **First-Party Benefits (medical):** at least **$5,000**.
- **Tort choice:** drivers select **limited tort** (lower premium, restricted right to sue for pain and suffering) or **full tort** (higher premium, full right to sue).
- **UM/UIM:** **not required**, but insurers must offer it; drivers may reject it in writing.

---

### Connecticut (CT) — At-fault (tort) state
**Minimum liability: 25/50/25**
- Bodily Injury: **$25,000 per person**
- Bodily Injury: **$50,000 per accident**
- Property Damage: **$25,000 per accident**
- **UM/UIM:** **required** at a minimum of **$25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident** (typically matching BI limits). Connecticut also offers an optional "conversion coverage" form of UIM.
- No PIP requirement (Connecticut is a tort state, not no-fault).

---

### Maryland (MD) — At-fault (tort) state
**Minimum liability: 30/60/15**
- Bodily Injury: **$30,000 per person**
- Bodily Injury: **$60,000 per accident**
- Property Damage: **$15,000 per accident**
- **UM/UIM:** **required**, at the same limits as liability (30/60/15). Maryland also requires **Uninsured Motorist Property Damage (UMPD)** coverage. UM/UIM **cannot** be waived.
- **PIP:** at least **$2,500** — but this **can be waived in writing**.

---

### Ohio (OH) — At-fault (tort) state
**Minimum liability: 25/50/25**
- Bodily Injury: **$25,000 per person**
- Bodily Injury: **$50,000 per accident**
- Property Damage: **$25,000 per accident**
- **UM/UIM:** insurers must **offer** it, but it is **not mandatory**; drivers may reject it in writing.
- No PIP requirement (Ohio is a tort state).
- Ohio also allows financial responsibility via a **$30,000 bond** as an alternative to a standard policy.
- Ohio's requirements are **not changing in 2026**.

## 12. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What states does NJM cover?**
A: NJM offers personal insurance (auto, home, condo, renters, umbrella) in New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Maryland, and Ohio. Its commercial insurance is available in a wider set of states, including New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Maryland, Ohio, New York, and Delaware.

**Q: Is NJM a good insurance company?**
A: NJM holds an AM Best financial-strength rating of A+ and has a low NAIC complaint ratio, along with strong customer-satisfaction and claims scores. Its main drawback is limited availability (personal lines in five states) and the lack of a dedicated mobile app.

**Q: What types of insurance does NJM sell?**
A: Personal auto, homeowners (HO-3 and HO-5), condo, renters, and personal umbrella, plus flood, motorcycle/ATV, and boat/watercraft through partners. On the commercial side: workers' compensation, commercial auto, the ProPack Commercial Package Policy, the ProEdge Businessowners Policy, and commercial excess/umbrella.

**Q: How do I file a claim with NJM?**
A: Call 1-800-367-6564 (available 24/7) or report online at www.njm.com.

**Q: How do I get a quote from NJM?**
A: Get a quote online at www.njm.com or call 1-800-232-6600 for sales and policy information. Commercial coverage is typically quoted through an independent agent.

**Q: What are NJM dividends and will I get one?**
A: As a mutual company, NJM returns profits to policyholders as dividends — a Regular Dividend credited at renewal and, at times, a Special Dividend paid by check. Dividends are not guaranteed, are not available on all policy forms, and are not paid in every state, so a dividend cannot be promised in advance.

**Q: What is the minimum car insurance I need in New Jersey?**
A: For a Standard Policy in 2026, the minimum liability is 35/70/25 ($35,000 bodily injury per person, $70,000 per accident, $25,000 property damage), plus at least $15,000 in PIP. New Jersey also offers a lower-cost Basic Policy with reduced coverage and little or no bodily-injury liability, which is generally not recommended.

**Q: What is the minimum car insurance in Pennsylvania?**
A: 15/30/5 ($15,000 bodily injury per person, $30,000 per accident, $5,000 property damage), plus at least $5,000 in first-party medical benefits. Pennsylvania drivers also choose between limited tort and full tort.

**Q: What is the minimum car insurance in Connecticut?**
A: 25/50/25, plus mandatory uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage of at least 25/50.

**Q: What is the minimum car insurance in Maryland?**
A: 30/60/15, plus mandatory UM/UIM at the same limits (including uninsured motorist property damage), and $2,500 in PIP unless waived in writing.

**Q: What is the minimum car insurance in Ohio?**
A: 25/50/25. UM/UIM must be offered but is not mandatory.

**Q: Is the state minimum enough coverage?**
A: It keeps you legal but is often not enough to cover a serious accident. Vehicle repair and medical costs frequently exceed minimum limits, leaving the at-fault driver personally responsible for the excess. Many drivers carry higher limits (e.g., 100/300/100) and add an umbrella policy.

**Q: What's the difference between comprehensive and collision?**
A: Collision pays for damage to your car from a crash; comprehensive pays for non-collision damage like theft, fire, vandalism, weather, or hitting an animal. Neither is legally required, but lenders usually require both on a financed or leased vehicle.

**Q: What does liability insurance NOT cover?**
A: It does not cover your own injuries or damage to your own vehicle — only the injuries and property damage you cause to others. Your own medical costs are handled by PIP/medical coverage, and your own vehicle by collision/comprehensive.
